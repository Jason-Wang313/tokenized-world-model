# Abstract

Tokenized world models compress continuous observations or states into discrete codebook tokens and predict future token sequences. This paper audits a selection-time failure: what happens when a planner samples `N` token futures and executes the one with the highest internal token score? We show in controlled tokenized/VQ settings that larger `N` can improve selected token likelihood while worsening real decoded utility when the codebook aliases physically distinct futures. We call this token-likelihood physical aliasing. We give an exact finite, tie-aware top-score selection law for the selected real utility curve and validate it by Monte Carlo. We then test token-specific repairs: codebook uncertainty, alias detection, decode-consistency checks, physical-validity filtering, rare-mode reweighting, and small pilot token-to-real calibration. These repairs substantially improve the selected high-N tail in the controlled settings. The results are diagnostic rather than universal: they do not establish benchmark-scale or robot deployment performance.
